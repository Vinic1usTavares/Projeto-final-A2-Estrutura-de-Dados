malloch building is a private residential apartment building on telegraph hill in san francisco designed in streamline moderne style and built in 1937 building one of best examples of its type in san francisco is also known as malloch apartments malloch apartment building and simply by its address 1360 montgomery street some have called it ocean liner house though other moderne buildings have also been known by that nickname designed by irvin goldstine for father son architects john jack s malloch and john rolph malloch building was used as a filming location in 1947 s dark passage a noir work starring humphrey bogart and lauren bacall design and construction building was intended as a home for jack malloch and his son john rolph malloch both men were partners in a father son architectural firm based in san francisco and both wanted to live on telegraph hill with a view of san francisco bay they determined to build an apartment which would provide them with fine dwellings and also with income from rental units it held irvin goldstine sometimes written irving or irvine designed building for them he had recently graduated from l ecole des beaux arts but had not yet earned his architect s license so he was not listed as architect of record instead mallochs were listed goldstine obtained his license in 1940 four years after he first sketched malloch building building site is on a steep hill so street level entrance at upper edge of property is not lowest floor four stories of apartments start at ground floor and go up and two more floors consisting of a 10 car garage and a sub basement storage space are below grade jutting out to east because of slope a manager s apartment was added in 1947 by extending steel beams from garage and suspending a two floor unit with steel cables structural engineer w s ellison oversaw construction of primarily wooden frame structure built atop a foundation and two utility floors made of reinforced concrete to meet exacting state requirements for stability thumb left upright open air lobby showing curved stairs and glass brick muralist alfred du pont also known as dupont was hired to design images to decorate exterior du pont produced two high silvery figures in or raised plaster on western facade of building and a third on north side du pont applied colored concrete to exterior and carved it into shape flanking main street entrance are two male figures image on right facing left is a spanish explorer with a telescope raised to one eye image on left facing right is a bare chested worker holding a globe towering above san francisco oakland bay bridge with sleek aircraft flying above and below him and ships moving in bay actual bridge can be seen from house it had been completed previous year when malloch building was under construction around corner from main entrance third silvery mural shows a robed woman spirit of california standing in front of california represented as a map building s elevator was designed and added to foyer after building was occupied it is encased in a backlit glass brick shaft at one edge of open air lobby scalloped steel railings line central staircase which is anchored by a glass brick column sandblasted designs are featured in glass plate windows above entrance interiors were streamlined as much as possible without traditional touches such as moldings indirect lighting was used throughout curves were abundant with rounded fireplaces topped with indirect lighting and a round dining room with floor to ceiling windows rounded balcony edges were set off by rounded bay windows bedrooms included circular dressing rooms with ample closet space in some of units glass brick was used as partition material city of san francisco notified mallochs that they were in violation of a building code prohibiting more than three floors above a garage because building contained four floors of apartments above garage mallochs successfully argued that their structure stepped back in progression up slope of telegraph hill so that top floor was not directly above garage no part of building was in violation of code use before building was completed it was fully rented in 1937 two malloch men moved into two penthouse suites in 12 unit apartment building collecting rent from other 10 tenants malloch building was featured in 1947 film noir work titled dark passage in film humphrey bogart playing an escaped prisoner is invited by lauren bacall into her apartment unit number 10 on third floor of malloch building in apartment bogart hides out while he heals from plastic surgery and plots to clear his name bogart wearily ascends nearby filbert steps in one scene on his way to malloch building modern day residents have been known to put a cutout of
