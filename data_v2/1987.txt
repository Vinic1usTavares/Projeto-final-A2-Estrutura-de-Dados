microsoft vs mikerowesoft was a legal dispute between microsoft and a canadian belmont high school student named mike rowe over domain name mikerowesoft com microsoft argued that their trademark had been infringed because of phonetic resemblance between microsoft and mikerowesoft case received international press attention following microsoft s perceived heavy handed approach to a 12th grade student s part time web design business and subsequent support that rowe received from online community a settlement was eventually reached with rowe granting ownership of domain to microsoft in exchange for an xbox and additional compensation background domain name mikerowesoft com was initially registered by canadian student mike rowe in august 2003 rowe set up site as a part time web design business choosing domain because of phonetic pun by adding word soft to end of his name microsoft saw name as trademark infringement because of its phonetic resemblance to their trademarked corporate name and demanded that he give up domain after receiving a letter from microsoft s canadian legal representatives smart biggar on january 14 2004 rowe replied asking to be compensated for giving up domain microsoft offered to pay rowe s out of pocket expenses of 10 original cost of registering domain name rowe countered asking instead for 10 000 later claiming that he did this because he was mad at microsoft for their initial 10 offer microsoft declined offer and sent a cease and desist letter spanning 25 pages microsoft accused rowe of setting up site in order to try to force them into a large financial settlement a practice known as cybersquatting press coverage and settlement rowe went to press creating publicity for case and garnering support for his cause including donations of over 6 000 and an offer of free advice from a lawyer at one point rowe was forced to take down his site after it was overwhelmed by around 250 000 page views over a period of twelve hours only managing to get site back up after changing to a service provider with a higher capacity case portrayed as a david versus goliath struggle by media characterized microsoft in a negative light resulting bad publicity was later described as a public relations mess public showing of support that rowe received was credited with softening microsoft s stance leading to an eventual settlement in late january 2004 it was revealed that two parties had come to an out of court settlement with microsoft taking control of domain in return microsoft agreed to pay all of expenses that rowe had incurred including setting up a new site at and redirecting traffic to com additionally microsoft provided rowe with a subscription to microsoft developer network an all expenses paid trip for him and his family to microsoft research tech fest at their headquarters in redmond washington training for microsoft certification and an xbox with a selection of games following an online poll rowe donated most of his legal defense fund to a children s hospital and used remaining money for his future university education further developments after settling dispute with microsoft rowe attempted to auction off documentation he had received on on line auction site ebay describing it as a piece of internet history materials included one copy of original 25 page cease and desist letter as well as an inch thick wipo book containing copies of trademarks web pages and e mails between him and microsoft auction received more than half a million page views and bidding rose to more than 200 000 high bids turned out to be fraudulent and auction was restricted to pre approved bidders after restarting from reserve price of 500 documents eventually sold for 1 037 microsoft later admitted that they may have been too aggressive in their defense of microsoft trademark following case it was suggested by struan robertson editor of out law com that microsoft had little choice but to pursue issue once it had come to light or they would have risked weakening their trademark this view was also espoused by zdnet who noted that had microsoft knowingly ignored rowe s site company would have risked losing right to fight future trademark infringements robertson opined legal proceedings would have made a strong argument for keeping his domain as he was using his real name and was not claiming to
