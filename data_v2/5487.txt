mortimer grimshaw 1826 22 december 1869 was an english political activist strike leader and cotton weaver he briefly attained national fame in 1850s due to his part in preston strike of 1853 54 a large man whose face was marked by smallpox he was renowned for his oratory which earned him nickname of thunderer of lancashire son of a radical public speaker and orator grimshaw s early campaigns were centred on improvement of working conditions for mill workers in village of royton and enforcement of factory acts he was an outspoken critic of whig party and an advocate of chartist movement in particular of their anti whig alliance with tory party strikes among mill workers began to break out during early 1850s and grimshaw was one of leaders along with george cowell of strike in preston in 1853 strikers were fruitless in their demands for a ten per cent wage increase and grimshaw found himself with no further role in workers unions he and his fellow conspirators were charged with conspiring to prevent people working in mills although charges were later dropped following defeat in preston grimshaw emigrated to united states where he became a supporter of confederacy in 1861 he and cowell attempted to intervene in a strike in clitheroe but were branded notorious scoundrels by weavers there for their parts in preston strike following year he joined a small group of lancashire men who unsuccessfully attempted to rally support for confederates in northern england after a period as a freelance industrial mediator selling his services to mill owners and working against unions he had once aligned with he returned to his job as a power loom weaver until his death from tuberculosis in 1869 grimshaw and his activities were basis for two of author charles dickens characters early life and career grimshaw was born in or around great harwood lancashire in 1824 or 1825 and was one of six children brought up in a working class family his father thomas had a reputation as a radical public speaker in early part of 19th century grimshaw initially worked as a cotton weaver before becoming a full time activist and agitator he first came to prominence in village of royton near oldham in 1852 as a campaigner for improvement and enforcement of factory acts to improve working conditions of those employed in cotton mills in same year he became editor of anti whig broadsheet royton vindicator he criticised suppression of local political activists and supported alliance of chartist movement with tories against whigs dutton king 1981 p 46 mill workers in england became increasingly disenchanted with improper practices by factory owners long working hours and unsatisfactory conditions during early 1850s workers throughout country began to campaign for higher wages and strikes were held in several towns and cities brickmakers in manchester struck in january while woollen mill operatives in west riding of yorkshire went on strike two months later grimshaw himself was involved in a strike in stockport where workers were demanding a ten percent wage increase in march 1853 preston strike of 1853 thumb grimshaw s friend and fellow activist george cowell speaking to gathered strikers in preston when cotton weavers in preston struck in september of that year again demanding a wage increase of ten percent he and fellow activist george cowell became most prominent leaders at mass meetings of workers grimshaw would often wear a white hat at meetings in style of politicians henry hunt and william cobbett according to local journalist charles hardwick he was a strong advocate of liberation of oppressed factory slaves but it was noted that his judgement in arguments could often be overpowered by his enthusiasm and emotions dutton king 1981 p 47 in seven months that grimshaw was involved with preston strike he travelled throughout lancashire and yorkshire speaking at more than 60 public meetings at one meeting in october 1853 he shared platform with john b horsfall and chartist leader ernest jones whose labour parliament grimshaw was involved in following year as one of two delegates from preston along with wallace labour parliament was partly a response to a lock out of around 20 000 mill workers by employers in preston its aim was to organise a levy on wages to support operatives who went on strike or were locked out those paying levy were to elect a committee to buy and look after land and factories and parliament would meet on a yearly basis however plan did not gain support among all chartists and plans never materialised preston strike ended in defeat for grimshaw cowell and operatives and as a result there were no suitable jobs for him in unions his characteristics would not have lent themselves to a secretarial role on 10 august 1854 pair were indicted along with nine other men for conspiring to prevent certain persons working in mills in preston although charges were dropped by prosecution judge presiding implied he would otherwise have felt compelled to pass down a severe sentence and expressed his hope that none of 11 would engage in such conspiracy again two years later grimshaw and horsfall raised funds to enable them to emigrate and he subsequently spent some time living in northern united states upon his return to england grimshaw cowell and two other weavers were involved in a dispute between mill owners and workers in clitheroe lancashire in 1861 however due to their lack of success in preston group were branded a gang of notorious scoundrels by strike leader john o neil and were treated with hostility by gathered crowds of weavers later career during his time in america grimshaw had developed pro confederacy tendencies in general there was some support for confederacy among people who were affected by shortage in cotton from america caused by american civil war such as traders and mill owners on a personal level grimshaw was opposed to growth of capitalism in united states and described notions of american freedom and liberty as farce and humbug in 1862 he became part of a small group of working class lancashire men all of whom had been involved in various strikes in 1850s assembled by liverpool based tin plate merchant james spence group held meetings with intention of rallying support for confederates within northern england particularly in lancashire and cheshire however when a vote regarding support of british mediation in america was taken at one meeting in blackburn only 12 out of more than 4 000 workers voted in favour of grimshaw local weavers association consequently declared themselves in favour of abraham lincoln and his union dutton king 1981 p 207 ultimately group s efforts were handicapped by an inability to maintain activity throughout duration of civil war by 1864 only two of original company remained blackett 2001 p 170 by end of his career grimshaw had become a freelance political adventurer industrial mediator and anti union agitator selling his services to mill owners and employers in an 1864 letter to george wilson former president of anti corn law league during 1840s he described himself as destitute and and begged lancashire and yorkshire railway chairman for money or employment this was last public record of grimshaw he returned to work as a power loom weaver until his death in from lung disease
