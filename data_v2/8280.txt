elizabeth casson 14 april 1881 17 december 1954 was a british doctor and an occupational therapy pioneer initially training as a secretary casson began studying medicine at university of bristol when she was 32 she received her medical degree in 1926 becoming first woman to receive one from university of bristol she also attained gaskell prize from royal medico psychological association and a diploma in psychological medicine from university of london casson set up a residential clinic for women with mental disorders at dorset house in bristol in 1929 with enough residential space for 800 patients and she opened first school of occupational therapy in uk in same building following year she took an active role in therapeutic activities at dorset house even playing a part in patient s production of pride and prejudice casson moved control of school to a non profit company in 1948 remaining as vice chairman and medical director she created elizabeth casson trust in 1949 intended to improve occupation therapy education and treatment in 1951 casson was awarded an obe for her work at dorset house and was also made an honorary fellow of world federation of occupational therapists a memorial lecture was set up in her name and psychiatric unit was named after her at callington road hospital biography casson known as elsie was born on 14 april 1881 in denbigh wales she was sixth child of a bank manager and amateur organ builder thomas casson from wales and his wife laura ann one of her brothers lewis casson went on to be an actor and theatre director whilst living in denbigh family encouraged seven children to enjoy arts in 1891 thomas decided to set up a business in organ building and family moved to london there casson was educated at st mary s college paddington before going to secretarial college at her father s behest after qualifying she worked as a secretary for her fathers organ building business until his retirement in 1908 casson was a working as housing manager for social reformer octavia hill taking care of living conditions of residents at red cross hall in southwark for next five years she organised recreational activities at hall focusing on arts octavia hill s philosophy influenced casson s work over following years in 1911 casson s uncle isambard owen vice chancellor at university of bristol promised her a place studying medicine if she could be formally matriculated to university she struggled with her latin exams for two years but eventually passed them and registered to study medicine on 1 october 1913 at age of 32 she graduated with her bachelor of medicine degree in 1919 and took on a role at west hertfordshire hospital in hemel hempstead there she noticed that patients in women s wards were enjoying using artistic talents and decided that such pursuits were important to treatment she realised that these pursuits were not only keeping patients occupied but also helping them gain self esteem and work through their issues casson became a medical officer at holloway sanatorium in 1921 developing an interest in occupational therapy whilst working there until 1929 in that time she gained a diploma in psychological medicine from university of london in 1922 achieved her doctorate of medicine from university of bristol in 1926 becoming first woman to do so and winning 1927 gaskell prize from royal medico psychological association she also helped set up standing committee in psychological medicine of medical women s federation and gaskell club for those recipients of prize dorset house in 1926 casson visited an occupational therapy centre in bloomingdale hospital new york city whilst on holiday and decided that uk needed a similar facility in late 1920s casson decided to establish one she borrowed ps1000 from her brother lewis to help set up occupational therapy centre dorset house opened in clifton bristol in 1929 as a residential centre for women with mental health issues she expanded dorset house in 1930 to include uk s first occupational therapy school known as dorset house school and became medical director herself school s course would teach not only medical subjects such as anatomy and physiology but also occupational treatments such as weaving bookbinding and other crafts as well as how to organise activities such as country dancing and how these activities applied to occupational therapy casson s vision for dorset house was that of a community where every individual was encouraged to feel that she had a real object having seen benefit in united states and at octavia hill she combined medical interventions with education and recreational approaches she actively involved herself in daily therapeutic activities often inspired by her artistic childhood and build closer relationships between patients and staff creating a community feel at one point she even cast herself in dorset house s drama production playing mr william collins in their 1934 rendition of pride and prejudice days would be structured so that activities would be planned throughout with occupational therapy carrying on into evenings and at weekends a typical day would include breakfast then flower arranging until 10 30 followed by an hour in occupation room for therapy after their work patients would go for a walk or join in community singing until 2pm between 2pm and 8pm there would be more time in occupation room a period of rest and time in garden at 8 pm there would be community games and country dancing casson believed it was important to keep therapy room separate from rest of department as it was specialised knowledge she also insisted that certain activities such as knitting were not used as it was not sufficient distraction from patient s fantasy system some concern existed that patients would not accept being put to work but of her 100 patients in 1931 only six refused therapy a member and later fellow of british homeopathy society casson also included homeopathic treatments in her programme for patients dorset house had space for 800 patients in residence during 1930s moving to bromsgrove during world war ii building was damaged during war so in 1946 school of occupational therapy moved to oxford whilst patients moved to clevedon to ensure costs were kept down for patients casson subsidised their treatment from her own funds she began to release control of projects in 1948 first by passing school to a nonprofit company with her as vice president and remaining as medical director then in 1949 casson created elizabeth casson trust which focused on occupational therapy treatment and education with majority of funds going to dorset house school in 1992 school became part of oxford brookes university societies outside of her work casson was a member of bristol societies she never lost touch with her welsh roots discussing similarities between wales and bristol with lord mayor at a meal in 1931 and becoming president of bristol cambrian society in 1934 she also became president of soroptimist club of bristol between 1938 and 1939 and was vice president of bristol branch of british social hygiene council in 1939 she was an advocate for women working in roles outside offices suggesting government grants for women who wanted to be nurses or teachers and giving lectures to parents about her work in occupational therapy in 1935 with soroptimist club she gave talks to her fellow members praising influence of octavia hill and explaining that she was interested more in mental health than physical health she advocated an ordered life and a happy atmosphere for everyone as vice president of social hygiene council she worked to ensure that people of bristol could give better sex education to their children without shocking them she did this in part by hosting a series of lectures called sex education and child investing profits from lectures in providing educational materials for those who could not afford them legacy in 1951 casson received an obe for her work establish dorset house and was also elected a fellow of world federation of occupational therapists she was considered a pioneer in field of occupational therapy casson died in bristol on 17 december 1954 after suffering with anaemia college of occupational therapists hold a memorial lecture in her name and callington road hospital has a
